Current status and NOAA/INSIVUMEH forecast — May 2026

As of May 14, 2026, the equatorial Pacific is in a neutral-to-El Niño transitional state with a +0.4°C anomaly in the Niño-3.4 region. NOAA issued a formal El Niño Advisory. INSIVUMEH calculates a 92% probability for the Jun–Aug 2026 trimester. NOAA CPC projects a 96% probability of continuation through the boreal winter 2026–27.

  • May (now): Neutral-to-warm transition. Rain onset may be irregular or delayed 1–2 weeks on the Pacific coast. Last harvest window likely available.
  • June–August: El Niño active. Rainfall 20–30% below average. Extended dry spell (canícula) of 8–10 weeks, possibly through early September — a key production opportunity.
  • September–October: Total rainfall remains below average, but individual events intensify. Higher risk of 80–100mm/24h extreme precipitation episodes.
  • November–December: El Niño maturing toward its peak (Dec–Feb 2027). Dry season starts earlier and stronger. Excellent end-of-year production window.

✦
The big opportunity of 2026: The El Niño extended dry spell (8–10 weeks) is the single most relevant event for the salt operation this year. In normal years the mid-summer dry spell lasts only 4–6 weeks and is rarely exploitable for production. This year the rain pause in July–August can constitute a full additional production cycle if ponds are prepared before July 1st.
⚠
The most underrated risk: Under El Niño, while total annual rainfall drops, individual extreme events can be more intense. A single 80–100mm overnight storm in September can dissolve weeks of crystallization in open ponds. Installing an early-warning precipitation alert system (INSIVUMEH app + local sensor) to allow 2–4 hours of response time is strongly recommended before September 1st.
