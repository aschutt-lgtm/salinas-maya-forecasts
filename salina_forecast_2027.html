⚡ ENSO context — El Niño 2026 → 2027

According to NOAA CPC (May 2026), there is a 96% probability of El Niño being active during the boreal winter 2026–27 (Dec–Feb). INSIVUMEH confirms a 92% probability for the Jun–Aug 2026 trimester. This means the 2026 rainy season — which establishes the hydrological baseline for 2027 — will already be under El Niño influence, with rainfall deficits on the Guatemalan Pacific coast and an extended dry spell potentially reaching into September 2026.

  • Dry season 2027 (Jan–Apr) longer and hotter: harvest window extended 10–14 extra days vs. average.
  • Mid-summer dry spell 2027 (Jul–Aug) potentially prolonged if El Niño persists, creating a second production window within the rainy season.
  • Intense but brief rains in the second half of the rainy season (Sep–Oct): higher risk of extreme precipitation events concentrated in 24–72h windows.
  • Temperatures 1–2°C above average during dry months: accelerated evaporation, favorable for brine concentration.
  • Tropical cyclone activity in the Eastern Pacific reduced in El Niño years (historically lower formation in the Guatemala–Mexico basin).

What does El Niño mean for Guatemala's Pacific coast?

El Niño warms the equatorial Pacific, weakening the trade winds and reducing the moisture that normally drives rainfall on Guatemala's Pacific slope. INSIVUMEH consistently documents 15–30% rainfall deficits on the Pacific coast during moderate El Niño events, with the mid-summer dry spell extending to 8–10 weeks (vs. the normal 4–6 weeks).

  • Pacific SST off Escuintla: rises ~1.3–1.4°C above normal at event peak (Dec–Mar).
  • 2027 rain onset: may be delayed 2–3 weeks vs. average (normally May), extending the dry season.
  • 2027 rain season end: possibly early October, shortening the late rainy season.
  • Second-half rainfall: more intense and concentrated in short burst events (risk of pond damage from high 24h accumulations).

Primary risk — concentrated extreme rainfall

Under El Niño, while total annual rainfall decreases, individual events can be more intense and concentrated over a few hours. On Guatemala's Pacific coast, the risk of >80mm/24h accumulations in Sep–Oct historically increases during El Niño years. This is the most operationally relevant risk for the salt ponds as it can dissolve already-formed crystallization.

Primary opportunity — extended dry spell

The 2026 dry spell is already forecast at up to 10 weeks per INSIVUMEH. This sets up 2027 for a potentially productive July–August window — something not available in neutral or La Niña years. Planning an additional concentration cycle during this period, with pond preparation beginning in June, can meaningfully increase annual output.
